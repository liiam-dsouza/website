## 1. Motivation

The Rivest-Shamir-Adleman (RSA) encryption algorithm is an asymmetric encryption algorithm
widely used across the internet in many products and services. Asymmetric encryption works on a
private key ‚Äì public key encryption system.

A person sending data over the internet would encrypt the data with their public key before sending
it off, and it can then be decrypted using the receiver‚Äôs private key. It is used in messaging services
that use end-to-end encryption such as Facebook Messenger, iMessage and WhattsApp. This case
study will look at the underlying computational algorithm responsible for RSA encryption ‚Äì the
current standard for data encryption on the internet.

Put simply, the motivation for the RSA encryption algorithm is ensuring the integrity of data being
sent over the internet.

## 3. Method

The implementation of the RSA encryption algorithm relies heavily on modular arithmetic, Euler‚Äôs
theorem and Euler‚Äôs totient function. To generate the public and private keys used to encrypt and
decrypt information, a user would use the following process.

1. The receiver chooses 2 large prime numbers, p and q. Their product n = pq will be half of
their public key.
2. The reciever then calculates (pq)=(p‚àí1)(q‚àí1) and chooses a number, e, which is relatively
prime to (pq). This number e is a number which is relatively prime to (pq), and is most
commonly 2^16 + 1 = 65537, however it could technically be as small as 3. It makes up the
second half of their public key.
3. The receiver would then calculate the modular inverse, called d, of e. This value d would be
their private key - which they would use to decrypt messages they receive.
4. Lastly the receiver would distribute both parts of their public key, n and e, so people can
use it to encrypt messages to send to them. However, they would keep their private key, d, a
secret and not share it with anyone. (Brilliant, n.d.)

Currently RSA is the standard for encrypting information on the internet as it is very hard to find
the prime factors of a very large prime number. Standard lengths of RSA keys are generally around
2048 bits for a high-strength key (IBM, 2021). Using classical computing methods available today,
it would take approximately 300 trillion years to break a 2048-bit encryptiion.

MIT mathematician Peter Shor developed Shor‚Äôs algorithm for factoring large numbers in 1994, a
theoretical quantum algorithm which would be capable of breaking an RSA encryption in a matter
of hours.
